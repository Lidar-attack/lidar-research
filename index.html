Reflective Deception: Exploiting LiDAR Vulnerabilities

Mirror-Based Attacks on Autonomous Vehicle Perception Systems

A comprehensive study revealing how ordinary mirrors can deceive LiDAR sensors, creating phantom obstacles or hiding real hazards from autonomous vehicles.

Abstract

Autonomous vehicles heavily rely on LiDAR sensors for accurate 3D perception of their environment. However, the physical properties of LiDAR make it inherently vulnerable to optical manipulation. This research investigates a novel class of passive LiDAR spoofing attacks that exploit mirror-like surfaces to inject or remove objects from the vehicle's perception.

Unlike prior work that requires active emitters or electronic tampering, our attacks rely solely on strategically placed planar mirrors to redirect LiDAR beams‚Äîposing a low-cost, stealthy threat. We introduce a comprehensive threat framework encompassing two adversarial goals:

Object Addition Attacks (OAA)

Create phantom obstacles by redirecting LiDAR pulses to generate false positive detections

Object Removal Attacks (ORA)

Obscure real objects by deflecting LiDAR beams away from genuine hazards

Introduction

The rapid evolution of Autonomous Driving (AD) technologies relies fundamentally on sophisticated sensor arrays that enable vehicles to accurately perceive their surroundings. Among these, Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) stands as a primary sensor, providing high-resolution 3D environmental information crucial for safe navigation.

The Vulnerability

LiDAR systems are inherently susceptible to deception from optical phenomena, including reflections and refractions. Mirror-like surfaces can redirect LiDAR pulses in unexpected ways, causing sensors to register non-existent objects or miss actual obstacles.

The Threat

A strategically placed mirror can trigger incorrect vehicle behaviors, ranging from abrupt emergency braking to dangerous ignorance of genuine obstacles, without any direct electronic tampering with the vehicle's systems.

Research Gap

While previous work has explored active LiDAR spoofing using laser emitters, mirror-based attacks remain largely underexplored as a direct adversarial vector, despite being passive, stealthy, and physically realizable using ordinary materials.

Experimental Setup

Our experimental campaign utilized controlled real-world testing to validate the mirror-based attack concepts under both ORA and OAA scenarios.

Equipment & Configuration

  • LiDAR Sensor: Ouster OS1-128 (128-channel, roof-mounted at 2.2m height)
  • Mirrors: Optically flat, high-reflectivity glass panels with silver backing
  • Test Environment: Closed parking lot and adjacent roadway
  • Data Collection: ROS 2 logging system for precise offline analysis

Attack Scenarios

Object Removal Attack (ORA)

Mirror positioned to deflect LiDAR beams away from a traffic cone, making the obstacle invisible to the sensor while the mirror redirects beams to the ground plane.

Object Addition Attack (OAA)

Mirror array angled to redirect LiDAR pulses toward environmental structures, creating phantom obstacles in the vehicle's perceived path.

CARLA Simulation Framework

We developed a physics-informed attack injection pipeline in the CARLA simulator to enable safe, repeatable testing of mirror-based attacks on autonomous vehicles.

Empirical Model Integration

Our system uses validated mathematical models derived from real-world experiments to synthesize realistic mirror-induced LiDAR artifacts in real-time during simulation.

Dynamic Attack Injection

The pipeline calculates geometric relationships between the ego vehicle and adversarial mirrors, dynamically injecting phantom point clouds based on distance, angle, and mirror surface area.

Real-World Validation with Autoware

To validate the real-world impact of our attacks, we integrated mirror-induced artifacts into a complete autonomous driving system running Autoware on a test vehicle.

Test Platform

Vehicle: Kia Soul EV
LiDAR: Ouster OS1-128
Software: Autoware.AI with CenterPoint detector
Test Mode: Full autonomous operation

60¬∞ Mirror Angle

System-Level Impact

Perception Corruption

  • False object detections with high confidence (74% for vehicle classification)
  • Occupancy grid contamination creating phantom obstacles
  • Sensor fusion inconsistencies between LiDAR and camera data

Planning & Control Failures

  • Emergency braking in response to non-existent threats
  • Path planning disruption causing unsafe stops
  • Traffic flow disruption due to phantom obstacle avoidance

Safety Implications

  • Rear-end collision risk from sudden deceleration
  • Traffic congestion from unexplained vehicle stops
  • Unpredictable behavior creating hazards for human drivers
